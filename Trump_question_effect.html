Trump question wording experiment

Professor Simon Jackman

United States Studies Centre, University of Sydney

17 July 2017

Respondents were asked to assess the influence of the United States on their country over the next five years. This item was administered with a “split ballot”, question wording experiment. A random half of respondents were asked to assess the influence of the United States on their country over the next five years.

Over the next five years, the influence of the United States on [country] will be …

The other random half of respondents received the same question, but with a reference to Donald Trump becoming president of the United States: i.e.,

Over the next five years - now that Donald Trump is President of the United States - the influence of the United States on [country] will be …

Seven response options ranged from “very positive” to “very negative.”

Randomization check

As with any experiment — but perhaps especially survey experiments — it is important to examine that assignment to the two forms of the question is in fact random. We do this by looking at the distribution of exogenous, pre-treatment variables conditional on assignment status. There should be no association between assisgment status and these variables. The following graph shows \(p\)-values from Pearson \(\chi^2\) tests of association between various pre-treatment variables and the assignment of respondents to treatment or control. In all but a handful of cases, we fail to reject the null hypothesis of no association between pre-treatment variables and assignment (at conventional \(p = .05\) levels of statistical significance, which are almost surely too liberal given the number of comparisons being assessed here), bolstering confidence that the randomization was successfully implemented in this case. There is a suggestion of imbalance in the India data, with respect to age and gender.

The 2nd graph displays the results of randomization checks with respect to continuous-valued, pre-treatment and/or exogenous variables: the observation number of each respondents, year of birth, start time and end time of the surveys. No patterns of association are apparent here.

Treatment effects, statistical significance

We continue the analysis with post-treatment variables, including the analysis of the item subject to the question-wording manipulation itself. The following graph presents \(p\)-values from Pearson \(\chi^2\) tests of association between subsequent variables in the survey and whether a respondent was randomly assigned to the ``Trump’’ version of the U.S. influence-on-country question.

The treatment’s effects appear to be largely and perhaps only confined to the specific item in which it was deployed. There is no systematic pattern to the sequences of \(p\)-values observed downstream from the treatment. A few of the \(p\)-values do trip the conventional \(p = .05\) significance level, but at rates that are consistent with chance and would fail to raise suspicion if we were to apply corrections for multiple comparisons. There is little commonality to the way that \(p\)-values do trip the .05 threshold across countries, further bolstering confidence that the effects of the question-wording treatment are confined to the specific item which was subject to treatment.
